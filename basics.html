What Are Cookies?

Cookies are small pieces of data stored in a user's browser by a website. Their main purpose is to help the website remember information about the user between visits or while moving from one page to another. Without cookies, many websites would feel less convenient and less personalized.

For example, cookies allow websites to keep users logged in, remember items in a shopping cart, and save preferences such as language or theme settings. They also play a role in analytics by helping websites understand how users interact with pages, which can be used to improve performance and user experience.

At the same time, cookies are not always limited to basic functionality. Some cookies are used for tracking and advertising purposes, which can raise concerns about how much user data is being collected and shared. Because of this, cookies are both a useful tool for developers and an important topic in discussions about privacy and transparency on the web.

How Cookies Work

When a user visits a website, the site can place a cookie in the browser. That cookie may store a session identifier, login state, language preference, shopping cart details, or other settings. When the user returns later, the website reads that cookie and restores the saved information.

Why Websites Use Them

  • Keep users signed in
  • Remember preferences like language or theme
  • Save shopping cart items
  • Measure user activity through analytics
  • Support personalized advertising

Types of Cookies

First-Party Cookies

These are created by the website the user is directly visiting. They are usually used for practical tasks such as remembering preferences or maintaining a session.

Third-Party Cookies

These are created by outside services connected to the site, such as ad networks or analytics tools. They are more controversial because they can be used to track users across different websites.

Session Cookies

Session cookies only last while the browser is open. They are often used to support logins and other short-term website actions.

Persistent Cookies

Persistent cookies remain on the device for a longer period. They help sites remember settings or previous activity even after the browser is closed.

Why Developers Should Understand Cookies

Cookies are not just a technical feature. They affect design, usability, security, and privacy. A web developer needs to understand both the benefits and the concerns connected to cookies in order to build websites that are useful and respectful to users.
